Momentum and Force

In order to change the momentum of a body [usually, this means changing its speed or direction], one must apply a force to it. But how much force?

Answer: it depends!
  • small force for a long time
  • big force for a short time

The amount by which an object's momentum changes is proportional to the product

(force) x (time) = Impulse

A large impulse causes a big change in an object's momentum

So we can figure out how long it takes:

6000 kg-m s right = (300 N right) t t = 6000 kg-m s 300 N = 20 s

Suppose that Fred and Joe join Bob, and each pushes with F = 300 N:

Now, t = 6000 kg-m s 900 N = 6.7 s

So a larger push yields a shorter time, as one would expect.

Ex. pushing a car

Bob's car has mass m = 1500 kg. It has run out of gas, and sits motionless on the road.

Bob gets out and pushes, exerting F = 300 N. How long does it take for the car to reach v = 4 m/s?

Initial momentum P i = 0

Final momentum P f = 6000 kg-m s

Change in momentum is P f - P i = 6000 kg-m s right

and the Impulse - Momentum Th34 states P f - P i = F t

When a force acts with constant magnitude over some time interval, it changes momentum by P f - P i = F t

Most real-world forces, however, change in strength over the course of an action.

In such cases, one must integrate the force over time: P f - P i = ∫ t i t f F(t) dt

Given a graph showing force as a function of time

the integral of force over time is simply the area under the curve.
