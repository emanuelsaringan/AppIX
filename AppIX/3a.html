Momentum and Impulse

Momentum - why bother?

You have already learned one quantity based on motion:

KE = 1 2 m v 2

Kinetic energy, combined with gravitational potential energy, can help you solve many problems, and predict the motions of bodies in many circumstances.

So - why bother with another quantity based on motion?

COLLISIONS!

It's very important to be able to figure out what's going to happen when objects collide … and simple energy methods don't work.

Linear Momentum

Fortunately, there is a quantity which remains the same, even when objects crumple or fold. We call it linear momentum:

P = m v

Note that linear momentum is a vector quantity: it has a direction, the same direction as an object's velocity.

(mass) (speed) = kg x m s

do not have any special name in the SI system.

Since velocity appears as the first power in momentum, but squared in kinetic energy, the two quantities can differ quite a bit.

Why does Conservation of Mechanical Energy fail? Two main reasons:

  • objects crumple, bend, break & deform when subjected to strong forces. These deformations absorb energy, removing it from the
    KE + GPE
    terms …
  • objects stick together sometimes after they collide - that changes the mass of each, so
    1 2 m v 2
    isn't simple any more.

It would be nice to find some quantity which is conserved, even during a collision…

Ex: Consider a bullet and a baseball:

bullet:
  • m = 0.008 kg
  • v = 440 m/s
baseball:
  • m = 0.2 kg
  • v = 40 m/s

The bullet has more kinetic energy,

  • bullet: ½(0.008 kg)(440 m/s)2 = 1549 J
  • baseball: ½(0.2 kg)(40 m/s)2 = 320 J

but less linear momentum:

  • bullet: ½(0.008 kg)(440 m/s) = 3.5 kg-m / s
  • baseball: ½(0.2 kg)(40 m/s) = 8.0 kg-m / s
